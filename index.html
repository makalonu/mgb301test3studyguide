MGB 301: Test 3 Study Guide (Spring 2026)

ORG. STRUCTURE

Structure: How job tasks are formally divided, grouped, and coordinated.

Key Elements

  • Work Specialization: Division of labor. Efficient use of skills via repetition, but can lead to boredom, fatigue, and high turnover.
  • Departmentalization: Grouping jobs together.
    - Functional: Groups similar specialties (efficiency).
    - Product: Accountability for product performance.
    - Geography: Best for scattered customers with localized needs.
    - Process & Customer: Based on flow or specific client base.
  • Chain of Command: Unbroken line of authority. Unity of Command: A subordinate should have only ONE superior.
  • Span of Control: Number of subordinates a manager can efficiently direct. Wider = cheaper/faster but risks effectiveness. Narrow = costly, complex vertical comms.
  • Centralization: Decision-making concentrated at a single point (top). Decentralized: Pushed down to lower levels (faster, more input).
  • Formalization: Degree to which jobs are standardized. High = strict rules, explicit descriptions.

Organizational Designs

  • Simple Structure: Low departmentalization, wide span of control, centralized, little formalization. (Startups).
  • Bureaucracy: Highly routine tasks, strict rules, centralized authority, narrow span of control. (Efficient for standardized tasks).
  • Matrix Structure: Dual lines of authority (combines Functional & Product). Breaks the Unity of Command concept.

New Structural Options

  • Virtual Structure: Small core organization that outsources major business functions. Highly centralized, little/no departmentalization.
  • Boundaryless Org: Eliminates chain of command, has limitless spans of control, replaces departments with empowered teams.

Two Models of Design

Mechanistic Model: High specialization, rigid departmentalization, clear chain of command, narrow spans, centralized, high formalization (e.g., Bureaucracy).

Organic Model: Cross-functional teams, free flow of info, wide spans, decentralized, low formalization (e.g., Boundaryless).

ORG. CULTURE

Culture: A system of shared meaning held by members. It is a descriptive term (unlike job satisfaction, which is evaluative).

7 Characteristics

  1. Innovation and Risk Taking
  2. Attention to Detail
  3. Outcome Orientation (Focus on results)
  4. People Orientation (Effect on people)
  5. Team Orientation (Work organized in teams)
  6. Aggressiveness (Competitive vs. easygoing)
  7. Stability (Status quo vs. growth)

Types & Functions

  • Dominant Culture: Expresses the core values shared by a majority.
  • Subcultures: Minicultures within an org, usually defined by department or geography.
  • Strong Culture: Core values are intensely held and widely shared. Creates high behavioral control and reduces turnover.
  • Culture's Function: Defines boundaries, conveys identity, facilitates commitment to something larger than self, enhances social stability, serves as a sense-making/control mechanism.
  • Culture as a Liability: Acts as a barrier to change, barrier to diversity, and barrier to acquisitions/mergers.

Sustaining Culture

Creation: Founders hire those who think like them, indoctrinate them, and act as role models.

Socialization Model:
1. Prearrival: Learning that occurs before joining.
2. Encounter: Seeing what the org is really like; expectations vs. reality.
3. Metamorphosis: Adjusting to the work, group, and org norms.

Conditions for Cultural Change: Dramatic crisis, turnover in top leadership, young & small organization, weak existing culture.

POWER & POLITICS

Power: Capacity A has to influence B to act in accordance with A's wishes. The key to power is DEPENDENCY.

Leadership vs. Power: Leadership requires goal congruence and focuses on downward influence. Power only requires dependence and is used in all directions.

Bases of Power

Formal Power: Based on position in org.

  • Coercive: Based on fear of negative results.
  • Reward: Ability to distribute desired rewards.
  • Legitimate: Formal authority to control resources based on structural position.

Personal Power (Most Effective):

  • Expert: Influence wielded via expertise, special skills, or knowledge.
  • Referent: Based on identification with a person who has desirable resources or traits (e.g., charisma, likability).

Influence Tactics

  • Rational Persuasion: Logical arguments/facts. The ONLY tactic effective across all organizational levels (especially upward).
  • Inspirational Appeals: Appealing to values/needs.
  • Consultation: Involving the target in the plan.
  • Ingratiation: Using flattery, praise, or friendly behavior prior to making a request.
  • Exchange: Rewarding target with benefits/favors.
  • Personal Appeals: Asking based on friendship.
  • Coalitions: Enlisting the aid of others to persuade.
  • Legitimacy: Relying on authority position.
  • Pressure: Using warnings, demands, threats.

Politics in Organizations

Political Behavior: Activities not required by one's formal role, but that influence the distribution of advantages/disadvantages within the org.

  • Legitimate: Normal day-to-day politics (complaining, bypassing chain of command, forming coalitions).
  • Illegitimate: Violates implied rules (sabotage, whistle-blowing, symbolic protests).

ORGANIZATIONAL CHANGE

Forces for Change: Nature of workforce (diversity), Technology, Economic shocks, Competition, Social trends, World politics.

Change Agents: Persons who act as catalysts and assume responsibility for managing change activities.

Resistance to Change

  • Individual: Habit, Security, Economic factors, Fear of the unknown, Selective info processing.
  • Organizational: Structural inertia (built-in mechanisms resist change), Limited focus of change, Group inertia, Threat to expertise, Threat to established power relationships.

Overcoming Resistance

  • Education/Communication: Fight misinformation.
  • Participation: Hard to resist a change you helped create.
  • Support/Commitment: Counseling, therapy, skills training.
  • Fairness: Implementing changes equitably.
  • Manipulation & Cooptation: Spinning the message or "buying off" the leaders of a resistance group.
  • Coercion: Direct threats or force (last resort).

Lewin's 3-Step Change Model

  1. Unfreezing: Overcoming the status quo by increasing Driving Forces (direct behavior away from status quo) and decreasing Restraining Forces (hinder movement).
  2. Movement: Actual shift to the new state.
  3. Refreezing: Stabilizing the change by balancing driving and restraining forces so the org doesn't revert.

Innovation

Idea Champions: Individuals who actively and enthusiastically promote an idea, build support, overcome resistance, and ensure that the idea is implemented. (They usually possess high self-confidence, persistence, energy, and risk-taking traits).

RAPID RECALL (EXAM TIPS)

  • Rational Persuasion is the best tactic for convincing your boss (upward influence).
  • Geography Departmentalization is best when customers are scattered over large areas with similar localized needs.
  • Work Specialization = most efficient use of employee skills via repetition.
  • Referent Power = based on someone's desirable traits/charisma.
  • Ingratiation = flattery BEFORE you ask for a favor.
  • Matrix structures purposely break the Unity of Command (you report to two bosses).
