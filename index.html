MGB 301: Test 3 Study Guide (Spring 2026)

ORG. STRUCTURE

Organizational Structure: How job tasks are formally divided, grouped, and coordinated.

6 Key Elements of Structure

  • 1. Work Specialization: Degree to which tasks are subdivided into separate jobs. Pros: Efficient use of skills via repetition. Cons: Human diseconomies (boredom, fatigue, stress, high turnover) if taken too far.
  • 2. Departmentalization: Basis by which jobs are grouped.
    - Functional: Grouped by functions performed (saves money/efficiencies).
    - Product: Grouped by product line (increases accountability).
    - Geography: Grouped by territory (Best for scattered customers with similar localized needs).
    - Process / Customer: Grouped by product flow or specific client base.
  • 3. Chain of Command: Unbroken line of authority extending from top to bottom. Authority: Inherent right of a managerial position to give orders. Unity of Command: A subordinate should have only ONE superior to whom they are directly responsible.
  • 4. Span of Control: Number of subordinates a manager can efficiently direct. Wider spans: Cheaper, faster decisions, empowers workers (but risks loss of control). Narrow spans: Better control, but expensive and complicates vertical communication.
  • 5. Centralization: Decision-making is concentrated at a single point (top managers). Decentralized: Decision-making pushed down to managers closest to the action. (e.g., Locos Tacos practice question). Results in faster action, more input, and higher morale.
  • 6. Formalization: Degree to which jobs are standardized. High: Strict rules, explicit job descriptions. Low: High employee discretion.

Common Organizational Designs

  • Simple Structure: Low departmentalization, wide spans, centralized in a single person, little formalization. (Common in startups. Risk: Inadequate as org grows; risky if reliant on one person).
  • Bureaucracy (Max Weber): Highly routine operating tasks achieved through specialization, formalized rules, centralized authority, narrow spans, strict chain of command. (Pros: Efficient for standardized tasks. Cons: Obsessive concern with rules, "silo" mentality).
  • Matrix Structure: Combines Functional and Product departmentalization. Purposely breaks the Unity of Command (employees have 2 bosses). (Pros: Facilitates complex coordination. Cons: Power struggles, role confusion).

New Structural Options & Models

  • Virtual Structure: Small core organization that outsources major business functions. Highly centralized, little/no departmentalization. Provides max flexibility but reduces management control.
  • Boundaryless Org (Jack Welch/GE): Eliminates the chain of command, limitless spans of control, replaces departments with empowered teams. Breaks down horizontal/vertical barriers.

Mechanistic Model: Synonymous with Bureaucracy. High specialization, rigid departmentalization, clear chain, narrow spans, centralized, high formalization.

Organic Model: Synonymous with Boundaryless. Cross-functional teams, free flow of info, wide spans, decentralized, low formalization.

ORG. CULTURE

Organizational Culture: A system of shared meaning held by members that distinguishes the org from others. It is a descriptive term (how employees perceive it). Job Satisfaction is an evaluative term (how they feel about it).

Org vs National Culture: National culture has a GREATER impact on employees than their organization's culture.

7 Primary Characteristics

  1. Innovation & Risk Taking: Degree employees are encouraged to be innovative.
  2. Attention to Detail: Expectation of precision/analysis.
  3. Outcome Orientation: Focus on results/ends rather than the techniques used to achieve them.
  4. People Orientation: Decisions take into account the effect of outcomes on people within the org.
  5. Team Orientation: Work activities organized around teams rather than individuals.
  6. Aggressiveness: People are competitive rather than easygoing.
  7. Stability: Org activities emphasize maintaining the status quo in contrast to growth.

Types & Functions of Culture

  • Dominant Culture: Expresses the core values shared by a majority of members.
  • Subcultures: Minicultures within an org, typically defined by department designations or geography.
  • Strong Culture: Core values are intensely held and widely shared. Acts as a substitute for formalization; provides high behavioral control and reduces turnover.
  • Culture's Functions: 1) Boundary-defining role. 2) Conveys sense of identity. 3) Facilitates commitment to something larger than self-interest. 4) Enhances social system stability. 5) Sense-making/control mechanism to guide behavior.
  • Culture as a Liability: Acts as a Barrier to Change (when environment is dynamic), Barrier to Diversity (pressure to conform), and Barrier to M&A (cultural mismatches kill mergers).

Creating & Sustaining Culture

Creation: Founders hire/keep those who think like them, indoctrinate them, and act as role models.

How Employees Learn Culture:
- Stories: Narratives about founders, rule breaking, past mistakes.
- Rituals: Repetitive sequences of activities expressing core values.
- Material Symbols: Office size, perks, executive dress code.
- Language: Jargon/acronyms unique to the org.

The Socialization Model (3 Stages):
1. Prearrival: The learning that occurs before a new member joins the organization (expectations).
2. Encounter: New employee sees what the org is really like (expectations clash with reality).
3. Metamorphosis: Employee adjusts to the work, group, and org norms.

Conditions for Cultural Change: 1) A dramatic crisis exists. 2) Turnover in top leadership. 3) The organization is young and small. 4) The existing culture is weak.

POWER & POLITICS

Power: A capacity that A has to influence the behavior of B so that B acts in accordance with A's wishes. The key to power is DEPENDENCY.

General Dependency Postulate: The greater B's dependence on A, the more power A has. Dependency increases when the resource you control is: Important, Scarce, and Nonsubstitutable.

Leadership vs Power: Leadership requires goal congruence, focuses on downward influence, and emphasizes leadership style. Power requires ONLY dependence, is used in all directions (down, up, lateral), and focuses on tactics.

Bases of Power (Formal vs Personal)

Formal Power: Based on an individual's position in an organization.

  • Coercive Power: Base dependent on fear of negative results (threats, firing).
  • Reward Power: Compliance achieved based on the ability to distribute desired rewards (pay, promos).
  • Legitimate Power: The formal authority to control and use resources based on structural position.

Personal Power: Power stemming from unique characteristics. Most effective source of power.

  • Expert Power: Influence wielded as a result of expertise, special skill, or knowledge.
  • Referent Power: Based on identification with a person who has desirable resources or personal traits (e.g., charisma, likability, respect).

9 Influence Tactics (Yukl)

Ways individuals translate power bases into specific actions.

  1. Rational Persuasion: Logical arguments and factual evidence. The ONLY tactic effective across all organizational levels (especially for upward influence / convincing your boss).
  2. Inspirational Appeals: Appealing to values, needs, hopes.
  3. Consultation: Involving the target in deciding how to accomplish the plan.
  4. Ingratiation: Using flattery, praise, or friendly behavior PRIOR to making a request.
  5. Exchange: Rewarding target with benefits/favors.
  6. Personal Appeals: Asking for compliance based on friendship/loyalty.
  7. Coalitions: Enlisting the aid of others to persuade.
  8. Legitimacy: Relying on your authority position.
  9. Pressure: Using warnings, repeated demands, threats.

Note: Substitution is a distractor on the test; it is NOT an influence tactic.

Politics in Organizations

Political Behavior: Activities that are not required as part of a person's formal role, but that influence the distribution of advantages and disadvantages within the org.

  • Legitimate Political Behavior: Normal everyday politics (complaining, bypassing chain of command, forming coalitions, networking).
  • Illegitimate Political Behavior: Extreme political behavior that violates implied rules (sabotage, whistle-blowing, calling in sick as protest).

Causes of Political Behavior:
- Individual Factors: High self-monitors, internal locus of control, high Machiavellian personality, high need for power, perceived job alternatives.
- Organizational Factors: Declining resources, promotion opportunities, low trust, role ambiguity, unclear performance evaluations, zero-sum reward systems.

ORGANIZATIONAL CHANGE

6 Forces for Change: Nature of the workforce (diversity), Technology (faster/cheaper), Economic shocks, Competition (global rivals), Social trends (online shopping), World politics.

Change Agents: Persons who act as catalysts and assume responsibility for managing change activities (can be managers, employees, or outside consultants).

Resistance to Change

  • Individual Resistance: Habit, Security (threat to safety), Economic factors (fear of lower pay), Fear of the unknown, Selective information processing (hearing what they want to hear).
  • Organizational Resistance: Structural inertia (built-in mechanisms keep things stable), Limited focus of change, Group inertia (group norms act as a constraint), Threat to expertise, Threat to established power relationships.

6 Tactics for Overcoming Resistance

  • Education & Communication: Fight misinformation.
  • Participation: It's hard for individuals to resist a change decision they helped create.
  • Building Support & Commitment: Counseling, new skills training.
  • Implementing Changes Fairly: Procedural fairness.
  • Manipulation & Cooptation: Manipulation = twisting facts. Cooptation combines manipulation + participation (buying off the leaders of a resistance group to get their endorsement).
  • Coercion: Application of direct threats or force (last resort).

Lewin's 3-Step Change Model

  1. Unfreezing: Overcoming the status quo by increasing Driving Forces (forces that direct behavior away from the status quo) and decreasing Restraining Forces (forces that hinder movement from existing equilibrium).
  2. Movement: The actual shift/transition to the new state.
  3. Refreezing: Stabilizing the change intervention by balancing driving and restraining forces so the organization doesn't revert back to old habits.

Idea Champions (Innovation): Individuals who take an innovation and actively/enthusiastically promote the idea, build support, overcome resistance, and ensure that the idea is implemented. Key Traits: Extremely high self-confidence, persistence, energy, and a tendency to take risks.

RAPID RECALL (TEST HITS)

  • Rational Persuasion = Best for upward influence (boss).
  • Geography Dept. = Best for scattered customers.
  • Work Specialization = Efficient use of skills via repetition.
  • Referent Power = Based on desirable traits/charisma.
  • Ingratiation = Flattery BEFORE asking for a favor.
  • Matrix structures purposely break Unity of Command.
  • Substitution is NOT an influence tactic.
  • Locos Tacos Example: Pushing decision-making down = Decentralization.
