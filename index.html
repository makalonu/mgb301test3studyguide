MGB 301: Test 3 Study Guide (Spring 2026)

ORG. STRUCTURE

Structure: How job tasks are formally divided, grouped, and coordinated.

Key Elements

  • Work Specialization: Division of labor. Efficient use of skills via repetition, but can lead to boredom, fatigue, and high turnover if taken too far.
  • Departmentalization: Grouping jobs together.
    - Functional: Groups similar specialties (efficiency/cost saving).
    - Product: Accountability for product performance.
    - Geography: Best for scattered customers with localized needs.
    - Process & Customer: Based on flow or specific client base.
  • Chain of Command: Unbroken line of authority. Unity of Command: A subordinate should have only ONE superior.
  • Span of Control: Number of subordinates a manager can efficiently direct. Wider = cheaper/faster but risks effectiveness. Narrow = costly, complex vertical comms.
  • Centralization: Decision-making concentrated at top. Decentralized: Pushed down to lower levels (faster action, more input, higher morale).
  • Formalization: Degree to which jobs are standardized. High = strict rules, explicit descriptions.

Organizational Designs & Risks

  • Simple Structure: Low departmentalization, wide span, centralized. Risk: Inadequate as org grows; risky if reliant on one person.
  • Bureaucracy: Highly routine tasks, strict rules, centralized. Risk: Obsessive concern with rules; lacks flexibility/speed.
  • Matrix Structure: Dual lines of authority (Functional + Product). Breaks Unity of Command. Risk: Power struggles, confusion.

New Structural Options & Models

  • Virtual Structure: Small core organization that outsources major business functions. Highly centralized, little/no departmentalization. Provides maximum flexibility.
  • Boundaryless Org: Eliminates chain of command, limitless spans of control, replaces departments with empowered teams.

Mechanistic Model: High specialization, rigid departmentalization, clear chain, narrow spans, centralized, high formalization (e.g., Bureaucracy).

Organic Model: Cross-functional teams, free flow of info, wide spans, decentralized, low formalization (e.g., Boundaryless).

ORG. CULTURE

Culture: A system of shared meaning. It is descriptive (how employees perceive it). Job Satisfaction is evaluative (how they feel about it).

Org vs National: National culture has a GREATER impact on employees than their organization's culture.

7 Characteristics

  1. Innovation and Risk Taking
  2. Attention to Detail
  3. Outcome Orientation (Focus on results/ends)
  4. People Orientation (Effect on people)
  5. Team Orientation (Work organized in teams)
  6. Aggressiveness (Competitive vs. easygoing)
  7. Stability (Status quo vs. growth)

Types & Functions

  • Dominant Culture: Core values shared by a majority.
  • Subcultures: Minicultures defined by dept/geography.
  • Strong Culture: Core values intensely held/widely shared. High behavioral control; reduces turnover.
  • Function: Defines boundaries, conveys identity, facilitates commitment, enhances social stability, serves as sense-making/control mechanism.
  • Liability: Barrier to change, diversity, and M&A.

Sustaining & Transmitting Culture

Creation: Founders hire those who think like them, indoctrinate them, and act as role models. Selection process weeds out misfits.

How Employees Learn Culture:
1. Stories: Narratives about founders/past events.
2. Rituals: Repetitive activities expressing core values.
3. Material Symbols: Office size, perks, dress code.
4. Language: Jargon/acronyms unique to the org.

Socialization Model:
1. Prearrival: Learning before joining.
2. Encounter: Expectations vs. reality.
3. Metamorphosis: Adjusting to work, group, norms.

Conditions for Change: Dramatic crisis, turnover in top leadership, young & small organization, weak existing culture.

POWER & POLITICS

Power: Capacity A has to influence B to act in accordance with A's wishes. The key to power is DEPENDENCY.

General Dependency Postulate: The greater B's dependence on A, the more power A has. Dependency increases when resources are: Important, Scarce, and Nonsubstitutable.

Lead vs Power: Leadership requires goal congruence & downward influence. Power only requires dependence & goes all directions.

Bases of Power

Formal Power: Based on position in org.

  • Coercive: Based on fear of negative results.
  • Reward: Ability to distribute desired rewards.
  • Legitimate: Formal authority to control resources.

Personal Power (Most Effective):

  • Expert: Influence wielded via expertise/special skills.
  • Referent: Based on identification with a person who has desirable resources or traits (e.g., charisma).

Influence Tactics

  • Rational Persuasion: Logical arguments/facts. The ONLY tactic effective across all levels (esp. upward).
  • Inspirational Appeals: Appealing to values/needs.
  • Consultation: Involving the target in the plan.
  • Ingratiation: Using flattery/praise PRIOR to a request.
  • Exchange: Rewarding target with benefits/favors.
  • Personal Appeals: Asking based on friendship.
  • Coalitions: Enlisting the aid of others to persuade.
  • Legitimacy: Relying on authority position.
  • Pressure: Using warnings, demands, threats.

Politics in Organizations

Political Behavior: Activities not required by formal role, but influence distribution of advantages within the org.

  • Legitimate: Normal politics (complaining, bypassing chain).
  • Illegitimate: Violates rules (sabotage, whistle-blowing).

Causes of Political Behavior:
- Individual: High self-monitors, Machiavellianism, internal locus of control, high need for power.
- Organizational: Declining resources, promotion opportunities, low trust, role ambiguity, unclear performance evaluations.

ORGANIZATIONAL CHANGE

Forces for Change: Nature of workforce, Technology, Economic shocks, Competition, Social trends, World politics.

Change Agents: Persons who act as catalysts and assume responsibility for managing change activities.

Resistance to Change

  • Individual: Habit, Security, Economic factors, Fear of the unknown, Selective info processing.
  • Organizational: Structural inertia, Limited focus of change, Group inertia, Threat to expertise/power.

Overcoming Resistance

  • Education/Communication: Fight misinformation.
  • Participation: Hard to resist a change you helped create.
  • Support/Commitment: Counseling, skills training.
  • Fairness: Implementing changes equitably.
  • Manipulate/Cooptation: Spinning the message or "buying off" leaders of a resistance group to get endorsement.
  • Coercion: Direct threats or force (last resort).

Lewin's 3-Step Change Model

  1. Unfreezing: Overcoming status quo by increasing Driving Forces (direct behavior away from status quo) and decreasing Restraining Forces (hinder movement).
  2. Movement: Actual shift to the new state.
  3. Refreezing: Stabilizing the change by balancing driving/restraining forces so the org doesn't revert.

Innovation / Idea Champions: Individuals who actively promote an idea, build support, overcome resistance, and ensure implementation. They possess high self-confidence, persistence, energy, and risk-taking traits.

RAPID RECALL (TEST HITS)

  • Rational Persuasion = Best for upward influence (boss).
  • Geography Dept. = Best for scattered customers.
  • Work Specialization = Efficient use of skills via repetition.
  • Referent Power = Based on desirable traits/charisma.
  • Ingratiation = Flattery BEFORE asking for a favor.
  • Matrix structures purposely break Unity of Command.
  • Substitution is NOT an influence tactic.
  • Cooptation combines manipulation and participation.
  • Locos Tacos Example (Practice Test): Pushing decision-making down to store managers = Decentralization.
  • Centralized Org: Top managers make all decisions.
  • Decentralized Org: Action can be taken more quickly to solve problems; more people provide input.
